Frequently Asked Questions

Technology and video game addiction are often misunderstood. Here is a framework for understanding what you or someone you love may be experiencing.

Is technology or gaming addiction a real problem?

Yes. When screen use becomes compulsive — crowding out sleep, relationships, work, and physical health — it functions much like other behavioral addictions. The World Health Organization recognizes gaming disorder, and growing research supports problematic internet and technology use as a legitimate concern.

How do I know if it's addiction or just a hobby?

Ask whether use continues despite clear negative consequences. Warning signs include lost sleep, neglected responsibilities, withdrawal from friends and family, irritability when unable to play or scroll, and a sense that life offline feels empty or unbearable.

Why can't I just stop on my own?

These platforms and games are engineered for engagement. Dopamine loops, social pressure, and escape from stress make willpower alone unreliable. Coaching provides structure, accountability, and strategies tailored to your specific triggers and life situation.

What does recovery look like?

Recovery is not about never touching a screen again. It's about rebuilding a life where technology serves you — not the other way around. That means restoring relationships, stabilizing work, improving physical health, and rediscovering purpose beyond the screen.

Who is coaching for?

Adults and young adults who feel stuck — whether you're a gamer who can't log off, a professional glued to your phone, or a parent watching someone you love disappear into a screen. If you're reading this, you're already taking the first step.

How to View Technology & Gaming Addiction

  1. Recognize the pattern — compulsive use despite harm to health, relationships, or responsibilities.
  2. Understand the mechanics — apps and games exploit reward systems; this is design, not personal failure.
  3. Identify what you're escaping — loneliness, anxiety, boredom, and stress often drive excessive use.
  4. Assess the collateral damage — map how addiction has affected work, relationships, sleep, and fitness.
  5. Set a north star — define what a balanced, fulfilling life looks like beyond the screen.
  6. Build replacement habits — fill the void with connection, movement, and meaningful goals.
  7. Create structure and accountability — boundaries, routines, and support make change sustainable.
  8. Progress, not perfection — setbacks are part of the journey; what matters is returning to the path.
